The Nagasaki bombing and The Little Boy


The Nagasaki bomb exploded in the city of Nagasaki. On August 9, 1945, the city was hit by a single 50 kiloton bomb dropped from a B-29 (a long-range, high-altitude plane). This bomb dropped on an inhabited city without warning. The Japanese military force on the harbor island in which the city was situated, had no chance to react.


The event itself was part of the broader firebombing of Japanese cities that followed the bombing of Hiroshima on August 6. Nagasaki was a significant source of Japanese casualties, with about 20,000 casualties. Nagasaki is also remembered as the first US attack on Japan, which the US military code named "Point C".

This was the first large-scale human extinction event due to _de novo synthetic nuclear explosive. There were about 60,000 killed in the blast and a citywide fire that consumed about 90 percent of the city.


It was in the aftermath that two of the key decisions made in bringing the war to a peaceful resolution were made.

Second, if doing so had still not been enough to establish peace, the US decided to use a strategic weapon on another city, Hiroshima, in order to establish de facto world peace. The Nagasaki Bombing and the Nuclear Age


The Nagasaki nuclear-armed bombs came out of a brief period of American isolationism that had been in motion for more than 50 years. The isolationist stance was in line with the American general desire to have no one dictate to them what they could and could not do. President Truman wanted to prevent the spread of communism and atomic weapons were part of that goal.


But isolationism ended when the Soviet Union invaded Germany in 1944. The bombing of Hiroshima and Nagasaki took place on August 6, 1945, shortly after the Soviet Union had liberated Berlin. The US administration wanted to establish the authority to use nuclear weapons in emergencies after Germany surrendered unconditionally to the Allies; Truman had approved the use of a few nuclear weapons in anger during the battle at Okinawa, with the result that the Russians would be left in control of the Japanese islands.


The Nagaki bomb was about a hundred times more destructive than the one dropped on Hiroshima. That made the Nagasaki bombing one hundred times more destructive on a country that had been under constant fire bombing for months. The United States was so confident that it was establishing its authority in the use of these weapons, the first time they had ever been used, that it dropped the Nagasaki bomb so early that there will be no survivors to protest it, even though the weapon was used as a means of coercion rather than self-defense, to establish its authority.

The United Nations (UN) was originally established to maintain world peace by preventing wars, but over time they had to broaden to include other issues, including how to handle nuclear weapons.


The Treaty of Non-Proliferated Nuclear Weapons was initially sponsored by the United States and the Soviet Union as a way of showing their strength and resolving the issue of the atomic bomb. Today, nuclear weapons have been established as a world safety issue, because nuclear weapons are no longer needed to establish world peace. Nuclear war is considered unsafe, and is prohibited by a broad base of treaty commitments. These treaties include the Atomic Energy Act of 1954 (the "Atoms for Peace" treaty), the Non-Profit Agreement on the Non-Nuclear Weapons, and various nuclear safeguards treaties such as the Partial Test Ban Treaty and the Non-Proximal Weapons Treaty.


On August 29, 1954, Dwight Eisenhower signed the Atoms for Peace treaty at the United Nations. Other signatories include the United Kingdom, the Republic of China, and France. It is now widely used as a negotiating and drafting document for more specific, universal treaties.


The first international treaty on the development of atomic weapons, and the control of their proliferation beyond two parties, was the Partial Test Ban of 1963. In essence, this treaty prohibited any country from using atomic weapons on any other country, without first seeking safe and lawful agreement from the signatory countries. It took some time for the treaties to spread, but over time it became the international custom (or norm, if you will) that no country would ever use them. They are generally considered a peaceful way of ending war without violence.
