Some evidence that IQ is genetically and hormonally heritable by Carlat and Flynn-Wright, 201 24 min read 2nd Mar 2022 0 comment Neuromorphic AI + Add Tag 7

Abstract


The study of heritability, or the degree to which intelligence is inherited, is used in research into genetics and to develop hypotheses about the causes of human variation.


There is also some evidence that IQ is a biological factor that could affect the likelihood of becoming a successful person.


We obtained a blood sample from 100 men enrolled in the study of intelligence, and measured their serum levels of testosterone and sex-hormone-binding globulin (SHBG).


We found that both serum testosterone levels (with 95% confidence intervals based on a normal distribution for each subject) and SHBG levels correlated with the results on the test of general intelligence (the reasoning section of the WAIS, the Symbol\xe2\x80\x93Digit substitution test, and the Visual Processing test).


We also measured levels of C3 complement protein C3 (Complement Factor C3) and C\xe2\x80\x93reactive protein (CRP). Again, higher C3 and CRP levels correlated positively with higher scores on the intelligence tests.


The pattern of results for the four variables is consistent with genetic and hormonally mediated variation in intelligence (which we term_ e.g. _"__Intelligence-Related Heritable Traits"). _


We also performed a meta-analysis on previously published research, examining the significance of these findings and the degree to which they differ by study design, outcome and trait studied.


We found consistent significant associations between individual C3, CRP, testosterone and SHBG levels. As far as we know, there has been no published research examining the correlations between IQ and C3 and CRPs (or, to our knowledge, any type of blood marker for inflammation or the major innate immune response).


We therefore report these new correlations, and attempt to explain them in terms of the known biology of the complement system. As our theory suggests for the biological basis for the heritability of intelligence, higher IQ would be expected to correlate with complement factor C3 and some forms of complement factor C3 (such as Factor H) lower serum or lower salivary concentrations of C3, and some forms of CRP are expected to correlate with intelligence.


Finally, we discuss the implications of this research for both research on intelligence-related traits and research into genetic risk factors for disease.
