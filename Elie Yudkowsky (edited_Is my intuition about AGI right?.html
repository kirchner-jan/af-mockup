In his classic article "In Defense of Epistemic Hipsters", Eliezer wrote:


Look, you tell me on a gut level that there's all this scientific controversy surrounding global warming...and I'll be the first to say "Ha ha! There's not scientific controversy surrounding global-warming-ism. Scientific controversy is the standard of proof for a false belief, and it's very clear that 'global-warming-ism' is true. It's been experimentally tested many times and confirmed."


Well, this is exactly the sort of situation where, not only do I not have a good example to offer, I literally can't name a single person in either the 'global-warming' or the 'global-cooling' camp whose opinion I respect.


My epistemic credentials are far more respectable. I have a Ph.D. from a respected university, for starters. But do I respect my credentials? I don't know. I suspect I do, a little. At least to some extent. But I also know _that I can make a case _a thousand times stronger for the existence of global warming than I can make for the existence of psychic powers, and yet, the scientific community has reached a consensus that global warming exists, whereas there is no scientific consensus on psychic powers.


To compare that state of affairs to a hypothetical world where (to make things even more extreme) I've heard that the scientific consensus is that psychic powers exist, and I can make a good argument, and no one has ever disagreed with my argument, then that is how you spot an epicycle in an epicycle. You would look at the community of scientists who believe in psychic powers, and consider whether they had a perfect right of their own to believe this. Or if not that, then a perfect right to believe that you had just spotted an epicycle in your own epicycle\xe2\x80\x94the perfect right to say "Yes, I can think of a case where the scientific community may be wrong\xe2\x80\x94_but not the _case I mean\xe2\x80\x94and yes, I know that's probably not what you mean\xe2\x80\x94but why not? Are you really that proud of your science?"


And this, I think, is a key part of my ability to argue the "Aha! An epicure!" response so much more effectively than most people, even most of the Rationalists I know.


(And that is, of course, part of why I was allowed to write all this at the age of nineteen. As well as, hopefully, why I was not allowed to publish it for a while.)


There is a common piece of anti-altruist propaganda that goes: "What kind of person can you be a good person if you think that just helping more will be the answer to saving the whole world?"


No. It just won't work. Not for one reason at least. When you hold the belief that helping just one more person could somehow be the key that opens up the floodgates to save the world, it just does not work.


It's not that I don't believe it. It's just that any belief in the power of helping just enough to save the world doesn't let me help enough to save the whole world. There's only one person in the whole world who needs this right now, and they may have already had it. There's only one person who's helping them save the world, and they might not like being thought of that way, but they also might not care what happens to them personally.


There's only one honest way to answer the question "If you could save the world, but only if someone else was helping in some small way, would you?"


It would be more effective to hold the belief: "I'm someone who helps when they can."


In terms of actions, not beliefs.
