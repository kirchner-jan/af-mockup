My previous post on the problem of teaching STEM fields in school was pretty long, so I created an infographic to summarize its points in as few words as possible. Some quick quotes from that post.


In schools, children from a low socioeconomic background are often encouraged to work harder on their math exams than those from a high socioeconomic background, even though the former group consistently does worse on the tests.


Math, physics, and chemistry tend to be perceived as the hardest and most prestigious of the sciences. In reality, physics and chemistry are at the bottom of the ranking on the percentage of subjects that are cited in research papers. (I'm not sure how to look this up.)


Science teachers often don't have a clue about how to teach physics, chemistry, or biology.


There is a huge demand for teachers, and it's very difficult to get hired without a PhD, and the best schools don't have good programs to train future teachers.


Mathematics is usually taught in high schools with teachers who want to signal that they can teach calculus to anyone who walks into the room.


Some of the main characteristics of mathematicians are obsessiveness, attention to detail, and an insatiable desire for new ideas. That's not true for most scientists. If new ideas are more important than the work itself, math majors become research assistants.

The Sequences


In the early 2000s, Eliezer Yudkowsky wrote some blog posts intended to teach you the meaning of life in a fun way. He called those blogs \xe2\x80\x93 as opposed to his more serious OvercomingBias blog \xe2\x80\x93 the Less Wrong sequences and in 2006 he published the best of them in book form, now called The Sequences. Many of Less Wrong's most prolific commenters got started by reading the sequences, so one of the more interesting aspects of the blog is the connections it makes with its parent blog.


In one episode we find Eliezer going through the sequences once again in the "How To Actually Change Your Mind" series.

I think that some of Eliezer's later posts have a lot to do with the community that grew up around the sequences on Less Wrong, but the above videos demonstrate that even I was surprised by how much of "What do you want to change about yourself?" is about Bayes' Theorem to its credit.

Probability___ ___is___ ___in___ _the map. It's an interesting philosophical and psychological topic that ties in to a bunch of other Less Wrong posts and is used heavily here. A more technical, mathematical, and empirical gloss on the topic, which I recommend is Yudkowsky's Epistemology of Disagreement.


Frequentist and Bayesian thinking are both good, and you can use both in situations where you expect to be right more often than wrong.


Arguments are soldiers. The more soldiers you have, the better. And just as it's good to have soldiers on both sides of an argument, it's even better to have soldiers on each side of an argument. Here is a quote from The Sequences I keep running into on LW:


I used to think the purpose of debate was to settle the truth. I no longer believe that. The purpose of debate is to win an argument. The more interesting and passionate the debate, the more intelligent and thoughtful the debaters, and the more carefully and publicly the debate is conducted, the more useful it is as an aid to the education of public officials or as an aid to collective persuasion. Once a debate is over, the only thing that counts is the final vote, and the winner will\xe2\x80\x94if the debate was honest, intelligent, and well-informed\xe2\x80\x94represent the majority view. [...]


Let me be quite clear on what I mean by being "honest": a debater cannot appeal to any statements made in "good faith" without thereby admitting those statements to be true. Even if the statements were made in honest good faith, the dishonest debater must still be punished; otherwise debate is not completely honest. There is no such thing as "white lies". But when I use the expression "honest", I have in mind something more precise than "sincere" or "candid". I am not talking about the debater simply being "true to his beliefs". I am also not talking about someone who only says things that they believe to be true. This is why you sometimes hear people complain about "dissent from majority opinion", if someone is accused of being "disliked by the majority" [emphasis mine]. As though being "discontinuous", idiosyncratic and rare, made you "respectable". In our society, being against the majority is something you can either get away with or not, but if you say it openly you're guilty of heresy, which is something the church can get you for.


If some politician says "I am a feminist", you don't really know if he has the courage of his convictions\xe2\x80\x94until you call him on some specific thing he said females have no rights to, or that some woman was raped on his watch, and you discover some embarrassing fact about his views on family courts. If you say this, he is not only "playing politics", he is being "dishonest", because if he truly believed all those things he would never have said them in the first place. Similarly, if you call someone a racist under any circumstances, or claim you are about to call them one when it is obviously false, or argue in a way that you know to be false, then what you're doing is being "dissonest"; if you truly believed it you would never have said it.


Belief is important, and you should take it seriously.


If you are going to have beliefs, it is important that they be true beliefs and not beliefs that are convenient things to believe. You need to have a good explanation for your belief, and you need to take other people's beliefs seriously, so that's why it is worth debating beliefs. There's a difference between saying "I believe in human evolution" and "this tribe over here believes in human evolution" is very similar to saying "I believe X", "belief (or knowledge or whatever) in Y" and "belief in human evolution" because you're not really making a statement about human evolution you are just saying "I believe this".


If you're trying to understand a topic deeply, there's no substitute for a solid understanding of the basics, so that is where the actual work of understanding is done.


__If you say the truth, even unpleasant truths, you will have power. __When you share good advice that a lot of people need but rarely hear, this gives you power. It could be the power to change people's minds, or the power to win arguments. Or even (though it's a much broader field of study) the power to create beauty.


As long as you share good advice, beautiful things will happen.
