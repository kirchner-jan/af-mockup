The UK Biobank is a study where 40,000 middle-aged British subjects have their physical and mental health tracked over many years.


It was found that women have slightly worse outcomes than men, with men making fewer major health decisions, even though they get more medical treatment and have slightly worse health outcomes in the short term.


This study wasn't a control group. It doesn't do randomized controlled trials. The sample consists of 40,000 people, but most of them participated in one study only. (Only 10k signed up for this study, only 6k people in total took on the project so far, 2k were lost, and there wasn't enough money to finish those 2k people.)


Their study design does allow for randomization of people to the intervention and control groups (it is a "quasi-randomized study") and they did randomize based on a lottery. This design is one that often has worse outcomes due to regression to the mean and confounding, but it does provide a way to evaluate the claims made in the study.


I am mostly interested in finding out what we can learn from this study, and figuring out what implications this information has for personal life and public policy.


Below I briefly describe each of the six components of the study, and break that down into their impact on personal life implications.


The Covid Tracking Project dataset which provided these information is not great, but I have been able to get some additional insights by integrating with their dataset. I'm happy to break down this further but it was easier for me to write it as is.


What are the key facts about this study?


Let's just recap the key findings:


The key claim is that women have slightly better cumulative _health outcomes than men, but men's _chronic _health outcomes are about equally good, and _short and long term outcomes are about equally bad for men and women.


The key study findings for you:


This article is about the short-term health effects. But why did I focus on those instead of looking at the _cumulative _effects of the long-term risks of COVID?


In a comment in this post, John_Maxwell says:


The long-term effects of COVID are the big win that everyone should talk about, but the short-term effects of the lockdown probably dominated the news for a few reasons. First it has a huge psychological component ("are you not afraid?"), and people aren't in general too good of statisticians so anything with a measure of impact has a higher share of attention. Second, it's something everyone can experience (the other big thing that can't be measured) and there will be lots of social pressure to experience that impact even if it's a small amount. Finally it's something that can be judged in a relatively short period of time, and also can actually be measured. In contrast, COVID-related long-term effects may still happen for everyone and may still be large, but they may be too long term or too hard to measure.

How did the study work


The main researcher study in question (cite: https://\xe2\x80\x8b\xe2\x80\x8bwww.thelancet.com/\xe2\x80\x8b\xe2\x80\x8bjournals/\xe2\x80\x8b\xe2\x80\x8blanres/\xe2\x80\x8b\xe2\x80\x8barticle/\xe2\x80\x8b\xe2\x80\x8bPIIS1373-3099(2021)30106-X ) had six points:


  • All adults who participated in the study in England could participate in full

  • All were given a laptop and given the relevant data tracking program and instructed to keep it in their own possession until the study ended on 4/\xe2\x80\x8b21st 2021

  • Incentive payment was given to participate in the study for any data (eg. blood pressure measurements taken) which was included in the data analysis.

  • A group of people on 4/\xe2\x80\x8b21/\xe2\x80\x8b21 were randomized to either being sent the information and consent form for the follow-up survey or not.

  • Information and consent were given to the first 10,000 people from this lottery group who sent an email requesting to participate.

  • The follow-up survey was sent out once per day to all the people in the randomization lottery.
