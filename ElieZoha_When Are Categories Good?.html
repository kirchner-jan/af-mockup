It is a fairly common mistake that one makes, not noticing that people have a different point of view from their own in some circumstances: "You said that X is not Y, which is surprising in and of itself, but what is it precisely about X and Y, such that you think that X is not a Y?"


It's often very difficult to specify clear and short examples even more, when it comes to abstract definitions


I propose a different approach.


1) We will imagine a world of two people.


B), who is a person with many interesting ideas.


A), who is a very smart and rational person, but one who seems to lack creativity and a tendency for original thought on some topics.


2) A first meeting between the two, in order to discuss A and B.


In our world, they could be friends, professors, or lovers


3) B) has a strong intuition and a lot of experience of A), being a thinker with different points of view.


4) B) proposes the following idea to A):


"You say that X is not-a Y", but what is X exactly, such that you can say that it is not a Y?


The question now appears to be very different from the previous one, in the sense that now people who have different experience and intuitions from each other can find common ground. (I would not be surprised if they can reach an understanding by going back and forth for weeks, rather than a single day)


But then what is this X that A doesn't think is a Y? When is it a Y, and when is it not a Y? This is in some sense the underlying problem of the entire question. And we could call this the meta-problems of a specific sub question. (For example, we could use: when does X have qualities that are more likely to bring Y as a whole, or when has X qualities that can be more independent of the whole)


We will call this the meta question.


In our example, the meta question could be "when a topic is a common property of X that is also known to have Y, do you think that X should be thought of as a Y?" Or "when a topic would be a common property of a topic, do you think it should be thought of?" or "When X has some properties to do with Y, do you have a higher probability of thinking of X in the context of a Y vs. not-a-Y?"


We assume that to some extent, a topic is a topic, because it's a Y, and to some extent it's not because it's not a Y, therefore the answer to this question will appear to be "sometimes, but not always".
