If you want to know what is going to happen next with a new group of people, that is usually much easier to negotiate with individual people, and then the individual people can easily know what will happen, and the rest of us know it too, because...well, there's a way you can say nice things about this part, too, but I'm not going to get into that.


What you can say nice comments about, is the fact that you have to spend more resources to negotiate with people, where common communication channels like phone, email and text become more costly. This is in contrast to the cost of a simple verbal agreement, which most people in most settings just don't bother with, such as an agreement to be in a car or on a bus with someone, because it's a hassle.1


However, common communication like this is the only _way to achieve coordination of a group of people with common goals, without explicit communication, which requires specialized skills. There's no alternative, you'd really just have to have a _huge pre-existing understanding and trust in people's intentions2, and all be on board, so to speak, which happens most of the time, but I think rarer than an observer might think. More discussion here3.


Common communication channels are thus expensive because you have to make them common knowledge and _trust _them when you need to use them to achieve coordination of many people\xe2\x80\x94e.g., a time on a bus or on a plane.


This makes it easy to compare costs\xe2\x80\x94e.g. the cost of a verbal agreement, or a letter to a friend or manager. If you do something costly and trust that it will work out, it's relatively cheap. That's the basic intuition for cost-benefit\xe2\x80\x94things worthwhile require expensive coordination.


This is related but different from how money and other markets work in the world. If I pay you one penny for your car or house, I don't have to trust you will return it if I forget to cancel my subscription or the loan or the mortgage or take out insurance... I can just throw away or lose the envelope with my name on it. In this sense, buying houses/\xe2\x80\x8bcars/\xe2\x80\x8bcars/\xe2\x80\x8bcars might be cheaper than writing friends/\xe2\x80\x8bacquaintances/\xe2\x80\x8bcompanions, and I would have no idea how expensive communication is, relative to house-buying unless I explicitly ask. If I tried it, though (and I have on occasion tried it), it still seems expensive, relative to the car purchases.


What's different about communication, though, is that as a communication mechanism, it should be possible to achieve coordination cheaply, given a high degree of shared understanding! You could have a verbal agreement for a bus trip (which would probably cost less in time and money than any of the people is paying directly for a bus ticket). But is there some way I can write a text, in addition to a verbal agreement, that would let both me and everyone else know that the bus trip is on?

Why do we do this? Why is communicating common knowledge and expensive and hard and so-forth?
