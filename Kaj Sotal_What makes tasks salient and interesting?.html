Types of Salience


I've long had the intuition that there are various different kinds of "saliency" signals in the environment. Here's a simplified version of the main types:


__Physical salience: __a type of salience that things can have independent of anything else in the environment. For example, a chair might be highly salient in an environment only because it was recently dropped in the room, or because some animal might be hiding somewhere in that visual field behind the chair that it thinks may be looking to eat it, whereas a random jumble of other items in the room might be much less salient in that situation. A chair might be salient to you in a new environment because it is very visible, whereas a tiny brown object might be very salient to you in your environment of choice but might not be as salient to the person next to you who is looking for a particular object.


__Actionable salience: __salience that emerges from something about an object, not from any particular environmental context around it. For example, I may move a cup of water across the room from where I'm sitting, or may pick up a small white stick and hold it up before speaking to someone with my other hand. The first action is more salient than the second because it emerges from my intention to do something I can easily control.


__Salient in terms of sensory intensity: __a salience signal that appears only when a thing makes sense in relation to your existing model of the environment. For instance, if you're in a room with a single other person, it may not make sense to talk to the other person very loudly so as to draw their attention, unless that person is the one you want a conversation with. In such a situation you may find it hard to draw attention in some cases because it doesn't make as much sense in relation to the other person's existing model of the world.
