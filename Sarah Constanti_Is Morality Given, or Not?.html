Here's what I wrote in my post on this topic, which you can read at [link] (I've added a couple of comments to illustrate the issues):


I sometimes get the impression that people think of morality as a priveliged property, sort of like weightlessness. There's a famous story from the 1700s about a ship foundering in a storm, the survivors of which prayed very earnestly to God to save them, and he heard them, and then they were rescued. You can almost hear that moral realism just has to be true in such a case, it being so obviously the case in other cases.


But it is not like that at all.


We use morality to carve up the part of the world that we share between people, to prevent the race to the bottom of mutual suffering, to facilitate coordination between people, and to have shared values that people aren't trying to manipulate (and they don't, if we value our rights).


In the same way that the shipwrecked people were praying to God because they thought that was better, or maybe a more effective, way to save themselves than just managing to paddle faster (because they, like God, were good at that sort of thing).


And you would think that the people who think moral realism is a priori obvious, would immediately realize that morality is not a priveliged property in this sense \xe2\x80\x94 you can't "take" morality and "add" it, as if morality being a priveligied property was an additional fact about the world, that has to be assumed. You can't get morality just by assuming that the universe is rational.


This is not an appeal that moral realism turns out to be false, or that morality turns out to be unimportant. The shipwrecked people, like God, would need to go where the evidence points, and figure out morality for themselves.
