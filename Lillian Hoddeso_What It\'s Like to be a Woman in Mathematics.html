In 1991 a survey was sent to all members of the Association for Women in Mathematics to gather information on:


(a) why they became mathematicians,


(b) problems which they encounter,


and (c) what they have learned from the problems they encounter.


The responses from over 150 women were compiled in this book by the authors

  • "I think that it's an easy thing, if you're just interested in mathematics. People look at women differently in maths than they do in engineering or other subjects. It's difficult for people in maths to imagine that women could be competent in maths. I would say, yes, people are competent, but we're not as good."

"Somebody like me had a talent, but the social skills were non-existent and the problem is that it's not a talent but rather a work ethic."


It is not by chance that all these surveys were conducted by women; there is a clear bias in who is willing to take the survey. The overwhelming response indicates a distinct perspective from those men who choose to be silent.


When we look at the survey responses (a), (b) and (c), we see that men and women approach mathematics very differently. Mathematician women generally have much more first-hand experience than mathematically-talented men. They go into engineering and non-mathematical subjects. And they often tell us that they really _like _to do mathematics, because it is just so straightforward, and they have the power and control over the subject for what they want to do it.

This is an absolute fact of life. Because women are in control of their bodies and it's impossible to get rid of a pregnancy or birth pains\xe2\x80\x94for that matter, it's impossible to control how many children you have; and also, it's a fact of life that women cannot hold down a non-menial job. Not all the women of math are this way, but I would say that the vast majority of women in math are as far as people go from being treated well. This article, on the "Gentoo Project" on gender diversity in math and computer science by Alexei Andreev says that:


According to an online survey carried out by Google Surveys it appears that 90.9% of the global population either likes math or wishes it liked math better. A majority of the female mathematicians like math more and more while men like math less and less. A sizable fraction of non-mathematiciarian women like math more and men like math less. The female mathematicians tend to be highly educated, and their non-mathematicinarian friends include a lot of mathematicians' spouses.


It's not just my impression either: this article claims that the ratio of male to female mathematicians has been going down. I suppose this is sort of a confirmation of what I've read from this survey, although to be fair, the survey is not as random as these articles. In reality, there are probably a whole bunch of un-sampled girls mathematicians out there who are really good at math and just didn't really sign up for the survey. (I wonder if this idea ever gets to Larry Summers?)


So the numbers are just not really representative of the distribution of women and men in mathematics on a wider scale. The data I'm using about men is just a slice from a whole pie (albeit a rather large slice). If mathematicians could be more like all of the other people in the world, I doubt it would be a problem.
