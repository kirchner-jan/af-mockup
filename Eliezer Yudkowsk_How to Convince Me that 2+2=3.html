(Followup to "The Math of Evolutionary Psychology")


Crocker's Warning


One day you get down on your hands and knees and scrub the dirt and chalk dust off the floor in the basement, and then you open up the cellar door. (Or look at the dust specks, or the way your fingers smell right after they've touched dirt\xe2\x80\x94you may not want to look.)


Because you've kept that door closed most of your life ("it makes me feel private..."), you don't remember how your brain looked when it was open.


For two years while you were learning to program you would do your programming in those basement rooms, and you would walk up to your computer for the bathroom, back down to the cellar door, and step into the light.


Now the cellar is an automatic reflex, and walking up to the computer isn't.


When your brain saw all those rooms stacked with boxes in the dark, it didn't have cognitive access to a picture of the contents of those boxes. So it didn't know how private it felt each time it opened its eyes in the darkness.


The cognitive science of the human brain isn't exactly settled, it may very well be that it's possible for a human\xe2\x80\x94especially a human raised in a completely non-visual sense\xe2\x80\x94that their entire vision of a room is just a picture of a room's contents. The room's colorless boxes might just be a real picture of the room's contents.


The brain goes on doing things that it can't see. "Can't see" is a relative term; your eyes go on staring at that same cellar door, over and over, for years. The human brain cannot actually fail to see something for the first time. Your brain is just refusing to process it for the first time, because it doesn't see a reason to.


So in one sense your brain's entire visual field is not consciously available to you. But in another sense the entire contents of the box you're carrying up from the cellar, are available inside you, to the brain's "perception."


Your brain is operating inside the cellar, and visualizing its own operation. If the cellar door has an image on its flat surface\xe2\x80\x94a picture of, say, a door that swings open\xe2\x80\x94then your brain may not know, until you open the cellar door and see the picture on the picture. But the brain can see pictures in its own visual field. And it is more than plausible that, in order to conceive that some visual scene is real, as opposed to imaginary, the brain may not actually _need to form an _explicit visual image. The brain may just need to form a visual mental image of itself looking at reality, such that the brain's cognitive system imagines itself seeing it.1


This is the difference between what you see yourself doing, and what you believe yourself to be thinking about yourself.


For example: If you believe that you are doing cognitive science, what you are really doing is visualizing yourself doing cognitive science. You have an image of yourself in front of the camera, looking at the screen on which it displays cognitive science, and you have an image of it writing its own program (and remembering how it wrote it). But your brain does not consciously recognize that it is in fact the screen that it seems to look at.


So that's another difference between what you think your imagination is doing, and what reality calls your perception: the imaginative picture forms in your head where you would see the physical screen.


But it would still be an error to conclude that your brain is doing only imagination. _You have an _imagined picture of yourself in front of an imagined screen, in the mind. 2 _You perceive it that way because it seems to be what's going on, not because the _physical reality is like that.


So if you can understand this, you'll understand that evolution can form a belief like "2+2=4" in your brain by changing the brain's visualizations to match physical reality... which you wouldn't actually visualize in the physical world, so that your brain wouldn't _actually _know about this.


And you say "But I imagine a tree growing inside an empty cellar, and a visual image of it." Do you really imagine it that way in your mind, or do you visualize the real thing that your eyes are seeing?

And by hypothesis, it seems like it might be possible to convince yourself that 2+2 really is 5, just by believing so firmly enough that you don't know what reality looks like except from inside the belief. I'm working with extremely simplified assumptions here.


It may be that the ability to convince one's self that 2+2 is equal to 5, is a necessary requirement for the ability to imagine that 2+2 might really be 4 or something...


Or if you're doing "logical" deductive reasoning, you may be able to visualize the steps leading to the conclusion, where in your visualization the logical steps appear to be valid. You may be able to visualize the way reality seems to be\xe2\x80\x94but not the logical way reality really is. Or maybe you can't actually picture that either.


But at least it may be possible to feel what reality actually does\xe2\x80\x94_to visualize what it _actually looks like _internally, whether you can do it by _imagining it, or by imagining yourself imagining it.


Imagine the brain does some of this "believing true things"\xe2\x80\x94then you may have a visual mental image of a brain that believes true things. But it seems plausible to me, for this type of process to happen, that what you have in mind, when you form this belief, is indeed a visual image inside your mind: a belief created by belief. This would just be another kind of belief about the state of reality, like any other. It would be a belief about the shape or content of the belief which you formed about reality, not reality itself.


It's entirely possible, then, to form beliefs such as "2+2 equals 5." The same brain that processes sensory input (in its non-conscious visual imagination) could conceivably just imagine _beliefs about the _shape and content of beliefs, in its imaginary visual field, rather than believing in the shape and content of any one belief.

4One way to imagine changing the visualizations to match reality, would be to _visualize _the internal mental states which caused the belief, and then visualize the corresponding real-world result of them.


In the future, you might be able to use "logic visualization" to visualize the result of "2+2" actually being "5", in conjunction with your explicit belief or knowledge or explicit visual perception of the result. By carefully and consciously visualizing it the way the math or logic is supposed to go, you can ensure it does go that way in your visualization.


It certainly seems possible to visualize yourself believing something, especially when trying to consciously direct your brain. But visualizing it can only go so far.


When we deliberately direct our thoughts, we do it by changing our intentions about what we're looking at. And changing your brain's intentions can only affect the visualizations which you have visualized in your own visual imagination.


4The visualization and imagination which we use by default, in forming beliefs and decisions, won't be very specific. This is because beliefs and decisions are too abstract; you can't put in all the details. It's too hard to _imagine the real thing, _when you're having to visualize that thing in your imagination.


So, for example, you can visualize a human being to be black, if you want to form a belief about that: but you couldn't _imagine the colorlessness _of a blackness that wasn't in your visual visualization.
