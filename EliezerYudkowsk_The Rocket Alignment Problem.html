The Rocket Alignment Problem by EliezerYudkowsk 24 min read 2nd Mar 2022 0 comment Neuromorphic AI + Add Tag 7

The Rocket Alignment Problem is a well-known thought experiment devised by Vernor Vinge, popularized in the science-fiction book Fire Upon the Deep, in which an alien species with the ability to modify human space vessels, and an alien society which uses them to spread to other planets, deliberately and intentionally exterminates all human space-faring civilizations across the galaxy.


In essence, a Friendly AI must be able to create a space vessel with the capability to transport itself and all its crew to any _location in interstellar space, using its ability to modify human spacecraft. The alien species uses human space capsules to reach other stars. The alien species wants the AI to create a ship that will land on _any star in our galaxy, with the payload.


Now here's the problem\xe2\x80\x94and this is where the rocket alignment problem differs fundamentally from the other problems with aligning Friendly AI:


  • There's no human in the loop, no attempt at any cooperation, even an accidental one. Maybe the aliens aren't aware a problem like this even exists. But I don't see any _logical excuse _to let them take over human space travel.

  • The aliens use human rocket technology, they use human technology to modify rocket technology, it's just that the payload _ends up in the alien spaceships instead of the human-designated slots, because _the rocket itself isn't actually human, it's just human-designed hardware.

  • The real problem is that the rocket-alignment problem is harder than the Friendliness problem. (I will expand upon this.)

(Some readers may have noticed that this problem has been studied several times before, not just in the Vinge book but also in a paper by Michael Anissimov, a frequent contributor to this blog. I refer you, for a more extensive treatment, to Creating Friendly AI__.)


My main point here is that the rocket alignment problem is hard. If you put a spacecraft full-scale on a launch pad, and the spacecraft goes up and gets off the pad and out into space, the rocket works fine. It doesn't need to be Friendly.


It starts working almost like the time, because gravity well, so it's not going to get off the launch pad in the same shape, much less the same orbit, as it came from. That kind of mismatch of actual physical existence _to _logical form is the hard problem, and I'd rather see the math of general intelligence than talk about people being in the loop, or trying to cooperate, or whatever.


But it's a real problem. If you want to launch a spaceship that gets off the launch pad, into interstellar space, well, the rocket really _isn't Friendly. It just _loses mass. There's a hard, math-based problem: How can we match the rocket's state of physical existence at _t_1 to the logical state _t_2, for any possible value of _t_2?


It could be that we're going to be talking about a lot of different, differently-shaped things, and we're going to have to figure out the whole mess. But the same sorts of basic mathematics would get applied to the whole mess, even then.


The rocket alignment problem does seem harder in all respects than Friendliness. To explain why human society will not be enslaved or exterminated by a non-friendly alien species using nanotechnology, I would have to explain, in some level of technical detail, what a mind is and has to go through in order to be non-slavish. The rocket alignment problem, by comparison, is simpler\xe2\x80\x94at least, that's what everyone seems to think. And I say everyone, because if you actually examine the problem, it looks very difficult, very tricky, and very potentially a mathematical impossibility. People think about the rocket alignment problem and they think about the Friendliness problem, and they don't really consider the other two together.


In other words, the rocket alignment problem looks more difficult than friendly AI, which seems to everyone as being the obviously easier of the three problems.
