III.


Why use the word "rationalism" at all, then?


Well, I'm guessing there's a story there. The origin of the word "rational".


"Rationalist" comes from the Greek word "\xe1\xbc\x80\xce\xbb\xcf\x86\xce\xb9\xce\xbc\xce\xaf\xcf\x83\xcf\x84\xce\xb9\xce\xba\xcf\x8c\xcf\x82", a-l-f-f-i-e-m-i\xe2\x80\x94literally, "one who pursues truth". To rationalize _something means to explain it away using facts; to "rationalize" something is to take it at its literal meaning and reject it for a bad-sounding or more useful-sounding interpretation. You can "rationalize" an argument ("It was really in the best interest of our company to fire X, and Y is a superior employee! You see, X was the best employee we had to offer, while Y has never worked in our company before! Well, they offered a higher wage, so he took it! So, X gets a better company, Y gets a job at a better company, I get a slightly worse company, the company profits $20 more, everybody wins!") or a belief ("You don't believe that, do you? But look at the evidence for what you're _already saying! The evidence for God exists! There's no way I can have an 'atheist' position and still believe that if I saw a burning bush I would think aliens did this!").


So if someone is _truly _pursuing truth, then they are a rationalist; if they say the universe was created in six days and you say it was created in 4 billion years, you may be mistaken about which one is more correct, but both parties are pursuing the truth. If someone makes a mistake, and rationalizes it away with a mistake-making logical move (like denying that the evidence in question proves anything because it only shows P versus\xc2\xacP), then they are being irrational. (I would define "irrationality" as "any explanation of data that you know, or should know in advance, you can expect a skeptic to be able to shoot down".) And if a person cannot make reasonable arguments, we should expect them to be a bad rationalist.
