Minsky's "Society of Mind" article as a rationality exercise by shminu 24 min read 2nd Mar 2022 0 comment Neuromorphic AI + Add Tag 7

The article begins to answer the question "Can intelligence have multiple realizability and be a single computational process?" The author goes on to show that a human mind involves many special purpose modules (SPMs) and that the cognitive process is the result of these modules performing cognitive labor, and not a product of them. He then shows that these modules are interdependent and thus, while there can be no doubt that they work together, it is not the case when we look at a specific module and try to identify what exactly it does. It would only become clear that it is how _it works, the underlying _theory of mind. This theory has to be agreed upon by these modules in order to work.


It is only at the part where the article goes into trying to construct a model of mind that it becomes possible to draw conclusions about rationality. The author's model is of course completely flawed and the modules are put there as an analogy to the way humans think (they are supposed to be like humans). But this leaves open the question of whether one could use the framework as a way of defining intelligence via how the components work together.


How could one go about looking at the current state of knowledge about the brain and applying the framework from this article to the brain, so that one could come to some of the questions we are still debating today? One of them is the neural implementation of the theory. I expect that it is possible and that this would be of immense value, even if only to lay out the known facts along the way and suggest where the gaps are.
