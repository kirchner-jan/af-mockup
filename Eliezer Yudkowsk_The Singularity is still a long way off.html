The Singularity is still a long way off by Eliezer Yudkowsk 24 min read 2nd Mar 2022 0 comment Neuromorphic AI + Add Tag 7

Once upon a time, people said, "But the future is so much more exciting!"


Once upon another time, people said: "But what would happen then?"


So you have to admit that the Singularity scenario is at least as exciting as saying that the first person who built a flying machine will live forever, right?


No. One of these two statements is true. It is not a matter of fact\xe2\x80\x94I mean, you know both statements. But neither is the stronger statement, which is that saying that the future will be as exciting as The Time Machine doesn't make the future any more likely\xe2\x80\x94it doesn't alter the laws of cause and effect.


So it's not so much exciting, is it? E. Y. said: "You know, the future's so much more exciting." This statement does not correspond to experience. The future's so much less exciting than The Time Machine. I am not just saying that things could be as exciting as I imagine them, because you don't know the imagined scenario, and it might be far more exciting. I am being precise here.


If you can imagine _experience with the singularity\xe2\x80\x94if you can see the future _in your mind's eye, which you have only imagined\xe2\x80\x94then we can talk about that as an event that has a chance of actually happening, you might say. It's not so much that this is your mind being the witness of the whole thing; but the claim is that you see the future and the future actually happens and you are in fact there.


But if you talk about "that has a chance of really happening," then you still have to do the standard step in analyzing future scenarios, and see how much more exciting they are\xe2\x80\x94how much more likely you think they are, how much more likely you are to anticipate them, how much more exciting their actual outcome might be. And when I said, "The future's so much less exciting than The Way Things Could Be," I do not mean just that we haven't invented superintelligent AI yet. I mean that, in the standard probability analysis of the singularity, it's the most unlikely scenario that you know about\xe2\x80\x94the least likely thing that's ever happened, so far.


"But that sounds like the sort of thing I do," I observed. "An unspoken intuition pump. The thing people use to generate future speculations, and the thing that drives them forward. It's a habit of thought."


"It sounds that way," someone said, "but it's also something your brain just learns to do automatically, so much so that it sounds odd if you try to say out loud 'This is a habit of thought I have created in my own brain; please tell me how I could have learned that habit of automatic thought, such that I now can do it even when I'm conscious.' It wouldn't make sense."


"Do you happen to know about a cognitive bias which makes people forget they are creating such a habit of thought? Can you name the bias?"


It was a good question, but I don't think that it had been named yet. So I answered it, and went on to the next question: "But of course you can say out loud how you know the thought. This is not the habit of thought you learned by thinking it automatically. You can say it out loud. You have remembered the original impulse, and put it back on autopilot for the question at hand."


After that, I noticed we were running out of time, and suggested continuing the conversation at some unspecified future location.


It seems clear to me, after the fact, that my own question-answering habit was not automatically going on\xe2\x80\x94that it didn't just happen; it was created. And I'm pretty sure it would have been different for someone else who didn't already have the question-answerving habit\xe2\x80\x94though to predict the exact thing you've just learned, you would have to watch yourself using it, if you actually saw the future in your own mind.


I do know of a technique that can, I think, be used to automatically put questions back on autopilot, by taking one particular element of conscious experience entirely out of your decision-making\xe2\x80\x94taking it out of your decision procedure\xe2\x80\x94so that you think you can't tell whether to do any particular conscious thing!


And this is one of the very keys to building Friendly AI. I will describe the theory later; for now it is just a reminder that your mind is made of the particular elements of experience that the mind is responsible for choosing to do.


There is a further key to building Friendly AI: How a single person can go from "I know what I am doing" to "I know that I am not making a decision"; and then how that one new thought about which to behave in terms of the expected utility of its terminal consequences, can take the place of the thousand half-thoughts that are running around in a human brain. This is described in a later post\xe2\x80\x94though it is not the core of the Singularity.


Then to go on from there, to building an Artificial Intelligence which is Friendly and does the job at hand, may require a little bit more creativity than I've shown so far.
