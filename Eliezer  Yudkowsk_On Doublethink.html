Followup to: The Third Alternative, Doublethink


The "Doublethink" spell is a useful tool for training rationality, that's why we talk about it on Less Wrong.


But if you don't understand the technique as presented here, the explanation can seem like nonsense. The example that first comes to mind is "you can't be mistaken about believing you're not conscious"\xe2\x80\x94seems like the problem is doublethinking about not being conscious. That may make doublethink seem impossible to do.


But this isn't really what doublethinking is about. It isn't about "believing in a lie". The illusion of consciousness is a natural consequence of a brain being made of atoms; it is not an error produced by believing in one. It is the brain that has the belief\xe2\x80\x94that is what "believes in consciousness".


The illusion of consciousness is an instance of not knowing about the map; it is the brain that sees the moon and the illusion of moon, and not the other way around. The only thing to be doubted is not knowing about oneself, not knowing that you are actually conscious.


The human capacity for rationalization is great and _it is very helpful. All the better to use when you need to make someone doubt their map\xe2\x80\x94and then use it to help make the map actually _correct. There's no sense denying "you know X", if "X" is false.


But rationalization is not the same as doublethink, because while the brain believes it is making a deliberate effort, actually, in a sense, it could always make an effort to rationalize all day.


In doublethink, the brain has gone from an actual map of a territory, to a verbal label attached to the map, to believing that that verbal label describes the territory, with no further step where it maps the verbal label within the territory.


In rationalization, the brain does not know the map has a verbal label, but keeps on believing it, in a sense. Why? Because it doesn't think there's any reason to change the map to match the territory.
