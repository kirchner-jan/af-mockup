When I was in sixth grade, I had read a book about the case of a serial killer nicknamed "the Green Boonville Monster," or, as it was called by the media when I was young, the Green Boonville Grissom Monster. The police, of course, thought that it was probably just some guy who liked to murder small children, and some nut, and he wasn't worth taking off his mask. But the media took it seriously. It took a couple of months, various people on the fringes would occasionally accuse me of having "boonville-monster" tattooed on my forehead, and the police would periodically ask me where I got it. Eventually the FBI came by and took my fingerprints, and then they kept asking me where they could drop the print, and I didn't bother to tell them. Then I read about the Boonville case and the "Boonville monster" and realized the people thinking it was a serial killer were exactly as unhinged as the people who thought that "boonville monster" meant that I couldn't possibly have gotten it from the library.

Boonville in Missouri was plagued by a series of murders, beginning with the murder of a five year old girl in 1958. The murderer kidnapped the victim while she was walking in a deserted town at night, and, since he found her in a deserted town, decided not to kill her. Instead, he tied her up, killed her, walked to town, and tied her body to her own body, and left it on the side of an abandoned factory.


The case went cold until the discovery of her remains two years later. She had been tied up in pretty much the same way as many of the previous victims, and the murderer wasn't all that hard to track down. The sheriff showed up to an old apartment full of garbage and took the man living there in for questioning. Later, they realized this guy might have also tied up his girlfriend, and asked her if she had heard from him. She said no. They searched the apartment and found her body, still tied and gagged, with her mouth caked with dirt. When they took her fingerprints later, they found that they matched the killer's.


A couple of days later, the newspapers blew open with another story. The victim had been found raped with animal feces in her eyes and mouth, like the worst sort of monster. Her face had been so horribly mutilated it was difficult to recognize her, her hands and feet had been gouged out, and her torso had been cut with an edged blade to make it appear she had been eviscerated. The investigation into the "boonville" cases ended there, at least until the next year. The sheriff told a reporter "you know, the only reason we didn't know is that there aren't any more of them," and then the case went cold for thirty more years.


But in the meantime, the legend had grown.
