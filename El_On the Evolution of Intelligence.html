A quote from The Idea in Evolution: A Theory of Why the Mind Sucks by Timothy B. Judge (2007):


 Some of the authors who have suggested that brain size is an upper limit to brain complexity, and that the human brain is relatively small in this regard, might have been taken in by a sort of anthropomorphism; the human brain might merely be a big brain _for reasons_. By analogy, a human brain might seem less bright because it is big relative to insects, and the fact that it is also big might be due to having its own innate mechanism by which it is dulled.

But why shouldn't larger brains be an advantage in an environment with very few problems to solve, but lots of problems to avoid? The more you have on your brain to work with, the more you can choose between different routes to get the same result.


Tl;dr;


If you wanted to go to the moon would you carry a huge bag of tools on your back? Or would you go to the moon with the essentials or something that a 3 person team was able to create in weeks?


No one can predict the future and we can't tell how much of the future will be created by evolution (of humans, animals or humans). The tools we have today aren't a guarantee of what we want to do 50 years from now.


The difference between evolution and a 3 person team is that evolution is an ongoing process as it works in new environments, so the information it has for us to use to change is much more accessible to us. If what we needed to do was change our DNA and our organs we wouldn't know to do that\xe2\x80\x94how would we notice if the tools we use today aren't the ones we want?


While evolution can work to make sure it retains useful information, that doesn't mean that it is doing a good job today of retaining our brains as they work today. It's still a question as to how much we want to know about our own bodies and minds as they are today. __


I want to think about my knowledge of intelligence, knowing that intelligence isn't really about being good at a job in the way that we think it is, when we hear the word "intelligence". What does intelligence feel like and is this something that can be improved on through learning?


I can't give a definite answer about how to improve on intelligence, but I might be able to give you a broad idea. (I say broad because I don't want to over-confidently say "I know".) I think intelligence might be about "finding good pathways. Making good choices." It's not like one of these things was invented just now, humanity has been doing these things for a long time.


"Being smart is knowing where to find things out in the world."


You can see this principle of intelligence as one of discovery or as a sort of knowledge of how to make things work. The more knowledge you have the easier it is to find solutions.


This is important because if we had a knowledge of what to do about a particular problem, then we would probably be able to find the thing a relatively short time after finding out about it, or even do it ourselves. __


On the other hand, if I never bothered to learn what IQ tests were, then when I finally got around to look them up, it might be only after I spent a while trying that I would find out what they measured. __


Or maybe I wouldn't do the research, I would ask someone and they would say, "there's a reason they called it IQ" and then I wouldn't know anything different than what they said, I wouldn't even be able to recognise if there's any evidence that the only reason this is so called is that most people think that it describes intelligence. Or maybe I'd do the research and see that it doesn't really. __


It would be too confusing to just have one set answers to questions. If I were a beginner at chess, I was confused about what to do when facing another strong player. Maybe a beginner in chess would know that the opening of a new game is very often very good, therefore I should explore the opening, maybe move the pieces to the right places to start with, maybe use my pawns to attack and so on. That would be better than just knowing opening or middle game. So how would I know if I was doing it right? Well, if I got it wrong would I just figure that out after the fact? Or might I even do research to make sure that what I was doing was right or not? Maybe I had no idea how chess works but if I did find out after while, it would be hard to figure out the right thing from there because the basic information is always evolving. (And the information we have today could very well be obsolete tomorrow).


I would also say: intelligence isn't just a measurement. "IQ tests measure you ability to get a lot of information into your head in a short space of time."


This seems easier, and I might be able even to improve my "IQ test scores" by improving my reading abilities or by learning to use new tools that help me to find information (more on finding information, later).


If you read a book from start to finish every day you might increase your IQ a lot because it gives you more information to put into different pathways. If you get more information per unit time then you can get more done faster. It might increase your effectiveness.


It seems that intelligence is made more efficient by being able to quickly store a load of information (information about what you are looking for and how to look for it) in your brain (your cortex) over time.
