There is a growing population. Most people are still relatively poor. But even on relatively more accurate predictions, like how soon we would run out of arable land or water or something, Malthus' track record was still pretty bad. Let's look at The Gift We Send To Tomorrow's predictions for things that Malthus could not have known.For example, let's follow Malthus' thinking on population growth. He writes, early in the book: "The population as we have hitherto inhabited the country, is the mean of a continuance of a uniform population or its first approximation, to which I have assigned the name Malthusian situation". So how well had people in the time of Gift done on this?Well, his predictions about population growth: they were mostly right! There was a good deal of immigration from Ireland and Scotland \xe2\x80\x93 and it did indeed have an effect "to raise the population." But overall, the British and American people were mostly in a Malthusan situation. It takes as much labor as can be found, and the population grows, but the labor supply does not grow with the population. It seems Malthus would have been surprised if a population grew at 1.2 percent per year but people still had a job. ____The only reason people don't run out of jobs is that lots of people want the jobs, so when there are not enough good jobs for everyone, desperate people go elsewhere to get jobs.And how did Malthus know the US and the UK would be so different? Malthus is famous (unlike many in his time) for his deep interest in the English countryside and his deep interest (on the side of England) in the American Midwest. And by Malthus' time these were some of the most dynamic and lucrative places for agriculture. So Malthus would have had strong reason to believe this \xe2\x80\x93 and for it to also have happened.In fact, Malthus would probably have been surprised if a country like Ireland that had not been devastated by the Great Famine of the 19th century had not had an influx of thousands of emigrants, who themselves were from one of the more dynamic economies of the world. It was the Irish Civil War of 1919-1921 that began the first large-scale population growth in Britain.The best thing about Malthus' book is that it gives us a way to judge whether people who disagreed with him were in fact wrong \xe2\x80\x93 by looking at how many predictions the author wouldn't have been surprised by later.If so many people disagreed with Malthus' prediction that "There is no limit to the multiplication of human beings", I'm not sure I'd need quotes from later commentators.


How about the modern-day world?Malthus was a Christian moralist, of course, and Christian moralities had to take a lot of moral-antagonistic conclusions as a result of the progress of science. He would have been surprised if not-for-the-sake-of-punishment. Malthus wrote: "To be able to feed and clothe a population of two thousand pounds sterling per head would necessitate one hundred and fifty-six million pounds six shillings and four pence, which is only seventeen thousand pounds a year per man". This works out to a hundred and fifty shillings per man per day. The median US GDP per capita was $39,000 in 2006, so that's $3.46 per man per day, or $15.94 per year.Now in the early twenty-first century, and with much more per-capita income than the average Malthusian in 1700, the cost of human welfare is much huger than in Malthus' predictions. And in fact we now spend about three times as much per person on human welfare as Malthus predicted.It may not be good that we had to wait so long for this to happen, but I guess it worked out for the best in the end.What would Malthus think of our society's ability to meet its own needs for food, shelter, and clothing?Malthus' book was published in 1798, before the industrial revolution had really started, and the cost of food had only really increased about three times. So I'd have to guess that Malthus would not have predicted the US would have so much food supply. But he would have predicted that more people would be so much better for farming.How often must I quote Thomas Malthus' Gift with those caveats attached?
