Is "X is real and important" knowledge? by cousin_i 24 min read 2nd Mar 2022 0 comment Neuromorphic AI + Add Tag 7
  • If P = (1, 1, 1, 4), then it doesn't matter at all whether P is a genuine fact about the world. To your goals, P is just some important thing about which you care, regardless of whether it's "genuinely real" or not.

  • If D = (1-x, 1-y, 1-z, 1/x + y/2 + z/2), then it's not clear that there's any way of deciding whether D is a genuine fact. But if, in a particular problem D is assigned, you can easily figure out the value of D without knowing whether it's "real" or not, then D is something you need to care about (i.e.: your goals and values are about D). For instance, if D has the value 3 /​ x, then if x is "real", then D is a genuine "fact". But if x is "fake", then D is not a "fact" at all, rather it's some number in the interval [0, 1] which is not meant to be relevant for your goals. There are two ways to resolve this conflict:

  • You can give up your goals and values and put them into the realm of "genuine" fake stuff. But this can hardly be the whole picture, because if you give up your goals, then you cannot decide whether a problem is important to you.

  • You can find some principled way to decide which questions are "genuine" vs "impostors." In short, you can do what you want with "facts of the real" and "fake but unimportant facts." This was the subject of this post. Let's consider some (very rough) heuristics for finding legitimate vs. bogus questions. This is based on my experience with physics, quantum mechanics and the history of science, and I welcome thoughts about other domains.

  • If a statement is clearly labeled and clearly about stuff you already observe, that's the right way to answer it.

  • If it's obviously unimportant for the stated purpose and it depends heavily on what you think, that's the wrong way to answer it (i.e. ask yourself what you actually want, not simply what "reality" wants).

  • If it depends heavily on specific values and goals, and you can't do it just by thinking about some abstract values, that's the correct way to answer it, because most of our values are actually goals about specific stuff — if you're not sure about these specific goals, think of something else (maybe you should set them aside now to get on with your problems).
